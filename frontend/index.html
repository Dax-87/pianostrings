Wire Gauge Systems
Three different standards worldwide — not interchangeable
European Standard
The most common system in Europe. The gauge number corresponds to the diameter in thousandths of a millimeter multiplied by 50. E.g.: No. 16 = 0.925 mm, No. 13.5 = 0.800 mm. Used by Steinway Hamburg, Bösendorfer, Bechstein, Fazioli and most Italian manufacturers.
N° × 0.0275 + 0.65 ≈ mm
Imperial Wire Gauge (UK)
British system based on the Birmingham Wire Gauge (BWG). Numbering is inverse: higher numbers = thinner wire. Rarely used today, but found in historical documentation of English pianos (Broadwood, Brinsmead). Not compatible with EU or US systems.
Inverse: ↑ No. = ↓ mm
American Music Wire Gauge
Two systems coexist in the US: the MUSIC WIRE GAUGE (for steel wire) and the WASHBURN & MOEN GAUGE (for copper windings). Steinway New York uses its own tables. When ordering US wire, always specify the system and exact diameter in mm or inches to avoid errors.

Technical note: The above table is sourced from academic references on piano string evolution and characteristics. Tensile strength values refer to certified quality wire. Strength decreases as diameter increases because thicker wires have less compact crystalline structure. For wound bass strings, the steel core diameter determines tension, while the copper winding increases linear mass lowering the fundamental frequency.

Recommendation: Always buy steel wire in the original factory packaging from manufacturers with certified processes. There is no unified numbering system worldwide — always specify the diameter in mm when ordering from abroad.
